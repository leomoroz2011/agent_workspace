# MODULE 0 — MINDSET (5 minutes, do this before every call)

## The One Reframe That Changes Everything

You're not trying to "convince" them to buy. You're a doctor. You're there to diagnose their problem and see if your solution fits.

If it fits → you tell them. If it doesn't → you walk away.

That shift takes the pressure off you. You're not desperate. You're a professional with a thing that works. They either have the problem or they don't.

## Say This to Yourself Before Every Call

> "I'm not here to sell. I'm here to find out if they have a problem I can fix. If they do, I'll show them the fix. If they don't, we'll both move on. Either way, I'm not losing anything."

## The Other Thing to Remember

Objections ("let me think about it", "what if it doesn't work?") are NOT rejections. They're buying signals. When someone has no interest, they just say "not for me" and hang up. If they're asking questions, they're interested.

# MODULE 1 — BEFORE THE CALL (Read once, drill until automatic)

## The 3 Rules

**Rule 1: Never mention price before the call.**
If they ask "how much does it cost?" before the call, say:
> "Pricing depends on what you actually need — I'll go over everything on the call and we can figure out what makes sense for your shop. When works for you?"

Never throw out a number before they've seen your demo. They'll judge you on price alone and say no.

**Rule 2: Send reminders.**
The second they agree to a time, send this:

> "Hey [name], locked in! Our call is [day] at [time]. Quick heads up — I'm actually going to have a demo of a website built for [shop name] ready to show you. See you then."

Then:
- 1 day before: "Hey [name], reminder — we're on for tomorrow at [time]. I'll have that demo ready."
- 1 hour before: "Hey, call is in an hour — I'll see you then."
- 5 min before: "Hey, just wrapping up — see you in a few."

The "I built something for you" line is key. Now if they no-show, they feel guilty about wasting your time.

**Rule 3: Never let them skip the call.**
If they say "just DM me the info" or "can you send pricing?":
> "I totally get it — I'll keep it short, 15 minutes max. I just want to make sure what I show you is actually useful for your specific shop. When's a good time this week?"

## Drill — Objections to Booking the Call

Practice saying these responses out loud until they feel natural:

| They say | You say |
|---|---|
| "How much does it cost?" | "Pricing depends on what you need — let's hop on a quick call and I'll show you what I built. When works?" |
| "Just send me info." | "I'll keep it under 15 min. I want to make sure what I show you actually fits your shop. When's a good time?" |
| "I'm busy this week." | "No problem — what about next week? Even 15 minutes would be enough." |

# MODULE 2 — OPENING THE CALL (The Most Important 3 Minutes)

## Don't Start Selling. Start Asking.

The first thing you do on the call is NOT show the website. It's ask questions.

Why? Because when THEY say they have a problem, they convince THEMSELVES to buy. You're just the doctor asking "where does it hurt?"

## Your Opening Line

> "Hey [name], thanks for getting on — I'll keep this quick. Before I show you anything, I just want to make sure I understand your situation. Mind if I ask you a few quick questions?"

They'll say yes. Now go into the diagnosis.

## The 5 Questions to Ask Every Barbershop Owner

Ask these in order. Let them talk. Don't interrupt. Don't sell yet.

**Q1 — Surface the review problem:**
> "Quick question — how many Google reviews does your shop have right now?"

(Let them answer.)

> "And do you know how many your top competitor in the area has?"

(If they don't know, you probably already do from your research. Tell them.)
> "I checked — [competitor] has [X] reviews. When someone in the neighborhood searches 'barbershop near me,' who do you think they're going to pick?"

They answer it themselves. You haven't sold anything yet.

**Q2 — Surface the website problem:**
> "Do you have a website right now?"

If yes: "When someone lands on it, can they book an appointment directly from it?"
If no: "So if someone finds you on Google at 11pm and wants to book — what happens?"

**Q3 — Surface the booking problem:**
> "How do most of your customers book right now — walk-ins, phone calls, something else?"

> "Do you ever miss calls or have people try to book when you're in the middle of a cut?"

**Q4 — Surface the missed money problem:**
> "Have you ever had someone reach out, you didn't respond fast enough, and they just went somewhere else?"

**Q5 — Confirm the pain:**
> "So it sounds like right now, if someone finds you online after hours or when you're busy, there's basically no way for them to book — they just disappear. Does that sound right?"

They say yes. That's your opening.

## The Transition Line

> "Okay — so let me show you what I built."

NOW you show the demo.

## Drill — The Diagnosis Roleplay

Read this out loud as if you're on a real call. Fill in the blanks with a real barbershop from your list:

> "Hey [name], thanks for getting on. Before I show you anything, can I ask a few quick questions? ...How many Google reviews do you have? ...And your biggest competitor near you — do you know how many they have? ...When someone searches 'barbershop in [neighborhood]' at 10pm, who do you think they're calling? ...Do you have a way for them to book right now without calling you? ...So basically, if someone finds you when you're busy or closed, they just move on — is that right? Okay — let me show you what I built."

Practice this until it takes under 3 minutes and feels like a normal conversation.

# MODULE 3 — THE DEMO (Showing the Website)

## How to Show It

Don't list features. Walk through what the customer would experience.

> "So here's what I built for [shop name]. Imagine you're a customer who just searched 'barbershop in [neighborhood]' — this is the page they land on."

Walk through:
1. **The look** — "First impression matters. Clean, professional, loads fast."
2. **The booking button** — "They hit this, pick a time, done. No phone call, no waiting. You get a notification, they get a confirmation."
3. **The reviews section** — "If you get to 50+ Google reviews, I embed them right here. New customers see social proof before they even call."
4. **Mobile** — "80% of people searching are on their phone. Here's how it looks." (Pull it up on your phone or resize the screen.)

Then stop talking. Let them react.

## The Only Thing You Say After Showing

> "What do you think?"

Shut up. Let them talk. Their reaction tells you where you are.

# MODULE 4 — STATING THE PRICE (The Scariest Moment — Here's Exactly What to Say)

## The Setup

You only say the price AFTER they've seen the demo and they're engaged. Signs they're engaged: asking questions, nodding, saying "oh that's cool," asking about how booking works.

## The Price Line

> "So here's how this works. I build you the full site — it's [your price, e.g. $300–$500 one-time]. That includes the booking system, the design, everything set up. You don't touch any of it."

Then: **shut up.** 

Do not keep talking. Do not say "is that okay?" Do not explain more. Just let the silence sit. The first person to speak after the price loses. Let it be them.

## If They Go Quiet

Wait 5 full seconds. If they still haven't spoken, you can add:

> "And if you're not happy with what I build, I'll refund you — no questions asked."

Then shut up again.

## Drill — Say the Price Out Loud Right Now

Literally say this out loud:

> "The full site — booking system, design, everything — is $[X]. You don't touch any of it."

Then count to 5 in silence. Practice not filling the silence.

Do this 10 times in a row until saying the price doesn't feel weird.

# MODULE 5 — HANDLING OBJECTIONS (Every One You'll Face)

## The Rule

Objections are not rejections. They're questions in disguise. Answer the question clearly, then close.

## The 6 Objections You'll Actually Get

**Objection 1: "Let me think about it."**

This means: "I'm interested but something's holding me back and I don't want to say it."

You say:
> "Totally fair. What part are you unsure about? Is it the price, or is it more about whether this will actually bring in more customers?"

Get the real objection. Then answer that.

---

**Objection 2: "What if it doesn't work?"**

> "That's exactly why I do the proof of concept. You pay half upfront — [half your price]. I build the whole thing. You see it before it goes live. If you don't like it, full refund. If you love it, we launch and you pay the rest. You only lose money if I do bad work — and I'm not going to do bad work."

---

**Objection 3: "I already have a website."**

> "Can I ask — does it have a booking system where customers can schedule directly? And when was the last time it was updated?"

If their website is old or has no booking: "That's kind of the problem — a static website doesn't bring you customers. This one does."

---

**Objection 4: "I don't have the budget right now."**

> "I get it. That's actually why I do the 50/50 — you pay [half price] now, see the full site before you commit to the rest. If money's tight, that keeps the risk low."

If they're still stuck: "What if we did [lower price] for a simplified version — just the booking page and contact info, and we build on it later?"

---

**Objection 5: "I need to talk to my partner / think about it with my wife / etc."**

> "Of course. Quick question — is there anything specific you're unsure about that I could clear up before you talk to them? That way you have all the info."

Then: "Would it help to hop on a quick call with both of you? I can walk through it in 10 minutes."

---

**Objection 6: "You're young / I don't know if I can trust this."**

(This is the one you're most likely to hit. Here's how you handle it.)

> "Fair — I'm 15. I can't pretend otherwise. But here's what I can tell you: I built this demo for your shop specifically. You can see it right now. Either it looks good or it doesn't — that's not about my age, that's about the work. If you like it and it brings you one new customer, it already paid for itself."

Then show them the demo again if they haven't really looked.

## Drill — Objection Reps

Have someone (or just yourself out loud) throw these at you one by one. Answer without looking at the script. Do this until every answer comes out automatically:

1. "Let me think about it."
2. "What if it doesn't work?"
3. "I already have a website."
4. "I don't have the budget."
5. "I need to talk to my partner."
6. "You seem young."

# MODULE 6 — CLOSING ON THE CALL (Exact Words)

## Book the Next Step Before Hanging Up

Never end a call with "I'll follow up." They ghost you.

If they said yes:
> "Perfect. I'll send you a Stripe link for the deposit — should be in your inbox in 5 minutes. Once that's in, I'll start building and have something to show you by [date]."

If they said "I need to think":
> "No problem. Let's put a time on the calendar now so you don't have to chase me down. What works — Thursday or Friday this week?"

Get them on the calendar before you hang up.

## The $1 Hold (For Free Trial Situations)

If you're offering a free trial or "try it first" deal:
> "I just need one thing from you — a $1 hold on your card. I'll refund it immediately, it just saves your info so when you're ready to launch, we don't have to go back and forth with payment details."

This gets commitment without requiring them to feel like they're buying.

## Drill — The Close

Say this out loud:

> "Awesome. I'll send the Stripe link over in the next 5 minutes. Once you pay the deposit, I'll get started and have something to show you by [3 days from now]. Sound good?"

Practice until it sounds like you've said it 100 times.

# MODULE 7 — AFTER THE CALL (Retention + Referrals)

## After You Deliver the Site

Check in every 2 weeks. Most people set it up and disappear — that's how you lose clients in 3 months.

Every 2 weeks:
> "Hey [name], just checking in — how's the booking system working? Anything you want adjusted?"

This keeps churn low and keeps you top of mind for referrals.

## How to Upsell

Once the site is live and working:
> "Now that the site's bringing people in — do you want me to set up a Google Business optimization so you start showing up higher in local search? That's the next thing that would actually move the needle."

You already have the relationship. Upselling is easy.

## How to Ask for Referrals (The Right Way)

Don't say: "Do you know anyone who could use this?" (Their brain goes blank.)

Say:
> "Do you know any other barbershop owners, salon owners, or solo service people in the city who might want something like this?"

Give their brain a category. They'll come up with names.

Add:
> "If you refer someone and they sign up, I'll give you 20% of whatever they pay. Completely up to you — just wanted to put it out there."

# DAILY PRACTICE PROTOCOL

## Every Morning (15 min before you make calls)

1. Read Module 0 (2 min)
2. Say your opening script out loud, start to close (5 min)
3. Run through 3 random objections from Module 5 out loud (5 min)
4. Say the price out loud 5 times with a 5-second silence after each (3 min)

## Full Roleplay (Do This 3x This Week)

Grab a friend, parent, or just do it solo by switching voices. Go through a full call:

1. Opening questions (Module 2)
2. Demo walkthrough (Module 3)
3. Price statement (Module 4)
4. One objection (Module 5)
5. Close (Module 6)

Time yourself. Goal: under 20 minutes for a full call.

## The Standard

You're ready to take real calls when you can get through a full roleplay without looking at any notes. Not memorized word-for-word — but the flow is automatic and the answers feel natural.
